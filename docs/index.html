Individual ship contribution to portfolio variance
Marginal variance contribution = ship’s individual variance × (1 + sum of cross-correlations weighted by open P&L share). Ships with high open ratios AND low cross-ship correlations contribute least to portfolio variance. Ships 2 and 5 (Brent-origin, high open ratio) contribute most individually but their HH decorrelation from Ships 1 and 6 provides the main offset.
★ Bottom line: The portfolio manages $84.30M of expected margin (sum-of-parts) but its actual risk profile is far superior to six independently-managed ships. The $4.74M std dev reduction and $17.86M VaR improvement come from HH–Brent decorrelation (ρ=0.15/0.05) — the fleet’s structural diversifier. v7 upgrade Regime-switching amplifies this: in stress, TTF–JKM drops to 0.40 making swing ships more valuable (+55% option value), partially offsetting the wider tail. The +$6.41M two-stage routing and +$4.66M CVaR uplift require active management (JKM–TTF spread vs trigger thresholds daily). Total: portfolio earns $10.45M more E[P&L] with $19.10M better CVaR than a naïve sum.

Key merger conclusions
CVaR improves by $5.66M — the biggest single benefit. Adding uncorrelated ME-origin swing ships provides downside protection the C1 fleet lacks.

Two-stage uplift increases by $2.45M — 5 swing ships (vs 3) means more re-routing optionality. The ME→Asia route provides a fast-cycle escape valve in stress.

Std Dev drops by $3.07M — portfolio variance reduction from imperfect correlation between ME-origin and US/NGA/AUS-origin margins.

Suez exposure increases — both C2 ships depend on Suez for EU delivery. Canal disruption now affects 5 of 6 Asia routes. Partial offset: ME→Asia avoids Suez entirely.

Net value of merger: +$5.66M CVaR uplift + $2.45M two-stage uplift = strong case for acquisition/charter.
